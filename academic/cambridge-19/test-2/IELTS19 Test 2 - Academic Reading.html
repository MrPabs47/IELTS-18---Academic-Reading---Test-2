The Industrial Revolution in Britain

The Industrial Revolution began in Britain in the mid-1700s and by the 1830s and 1840s had spread to many other parts of the world, including the United States. In Britain, it was a period when a largely rural, agrarian society was transformed into an industrialised, urban one.

The foundations of the Industrial Revolution date back to the early 1700s, when the English inventor Thomas Newcomen designed the first modern steam engine... Watt later worked with Matthew Boulton to invent a new steam engine driven by both the forward and backward strokes of the piston.

The demand for coal grew rapidly during the Industrial Revolution, as it was needed to run factories and steam-powered transportation.

Before the Industrial Revolution, the textile business was a ‘cottage industry’, with work performed in small workshops or homes. With new machines, relatively little labour was required.

The British iron industry adopted the smelting of iron ore with coke, producing higher-quality metals. Demand expanded with railway growth.

In 1837, William Cooke and Charles Wheatstone patented the first commercial telegraphy system, soon used for railway signalling.

Industrialisation accelerated urbanisation, but overcrowded cities suffered pollution and inadequate sanitation.

Although industrialisation increased overall output, many workers faced long hours, dangerous conditions and low wages. Luddite resistance eventually faded by 1813.

Athletes and stress

A It isn’t easy being a professional athlete... Emma Raducanu wrote about her 2021 Wimbledon withdrawal and attributed symptoms to excitement and buzz.

B Performance stress reflects demands versus resources, including concerns about injury and perceived control/confidence.

C Challenge states generally improve performance while threat states worsen performance.

D Adrenaline and cortisol shape stress responses; in tennis, cortisol is associated with more unsuccessful serves and anxiety.

E Anxiety intensity depends on demands/resources and repeated episodes with negative responses can harm health.

F Coaches/parents and psychologists can help reframe stress; visualisation and pressure simulation can reduce threat responses.

An inquiry into the existence of the gifted child

Maryam Mirzakhani, the only female Fields Medal winner, was not obviously gifted in childhood and became interested in maths after being intrigued by a puzzle.

Her curiosity, persistence, and discovery-driven work led to innovative mathematics.

Similar patterns appear in other high achievers; many were unexceptional early on. Research suggests performance depends on learned behaviours, support, and deliberate practice.

Deborah Eyre and Anders Ericsson argue that high-level performance can be developed through attitudes, hard work, and sustained practice rather than innate gifts alone.

Bloom’s findings highlight parental/adult support and strong work ethic in high achievers, including disadvantaged children supported by adults valuing education.

Einstein emphasized character and persistence over pure intellect.
